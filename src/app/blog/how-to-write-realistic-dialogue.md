# How to Write Realistic Dialogue: 12 Techniques for Natural-Sounding Conversations

Great dialogue does more than just move the plot forward—it reveals character, creates subtext, and makes your fictional world feel real. But writing dialogue that sounds natural without being boring is one of the hardest skills for writers to master.

According to publishing industry data, **dialogue accounts for 40-60% of most contemporary fiction**. Getting it right isn't optional—it's essential.

Drawing from dialogue experts, bestselling authors, and scriptwriting techniques, these **12 proven methods** will help you craft conversations that readers will believe, remember, and quote.

## 1. Listen to Real Conversations

The best dialogue writers are excellent listeners. Pay attention to how people actually talk.

**What to listen for:**
- **Interruptions** and overlapping speech
- **Filler words** (um, like, you know)
- **Incomplete sentences** and fragmented thoughts
- **Changes in topic** and non-sequiturs
- **Verbal tics** and personal phrases

**Real conversation patterns:**
- People rarely speak in complete, grammatical sentences
- Conversations jump between topics
- Listeners react emotionally before logically
- Power dynamics affect who speaks and when

**Aevon implementation:**
Use the **Quick Scratchpad** to jot down real conversations you overhear. Create **Character Attributes** for verbal tics and speech patterns specific to each character.

## 2. Give Each Character a Unique Voice

Every character should sound different. Dialogue should reveal who's speaking without attribution.

**Elements of unique voice:**
- **Vocabulary** (formal, casual, technical, simple)
- **Sentence structure** (short and choppy vs. long and complex)
- **Rhythm and pace** (fast talker vs. deliberate speaker)
- **Favorite phrases** and verbal habits
- **Emotional triggers** (what makes them talk differently)

**Example: Harry Potter series**
Hagrid's rough, affectionate speech ("I shouldn't have said that") differs completely from Hermione's precise, knowledgeable dialogue ("It's levi-O-sa, not levio-SAR").

**Character voice development in Aevon:**

- **Personality field**: How personality affects speech patterns
- **Motivation field**: What topics make them passionate or defensive
- **Appearance and background**: How education and experience shape vocabulary
- **Notes field**: Specific verbal tics and favorite phrases

## 3. Use Subtext and Unsaid Things

What characters DON'T say is often more important than what they do say.

**Creating subtext:**
- **Avoidance**: Characters change subject to avoid uncomfortable topics
- **Understatement**: Downplaying emotions or events
- **Implication**: Suggesting without stating directly
- **Contradiction**: Saying one thing while meaning another

**Example: Pride and Prejudice**
When Mr. Darcy says, "She is tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me," the subtext reveals his growing attraction and social prejudice.

**Subtext tracking in Aevon:**
Use the **Character Attributes** notes field to document:
- **What characters avoid discussing**
- **Hidden meanings** behind their words
- **Emotional subtext** in conversations
- **Relationship dynamics** affecting speech

## 4. Show, Don't Tell Emotions

Instead of saying "she was angry," show it through dialogue.

**Emotional dialogue techniques:**
- **Word choice**: Angry characters use sharp, aggressive words
- **Sentence length**: Frustrated characters use short, clipped sentences
- **Interruptions**: Impatient characters cut others off
- **Questions**: Confused or suspicious characters question everything

**Example: The Great Gatsby**
When Tom discovers Daisy's affair, his dialogue becomes increasingly aggressive and fragmented: " 'You're crazy!' he exploded. 'I can't say what happened in that room. I can't say—' "

**Emotion mapping in Aevon:**
Use the **Character Arc field** to track how dialogue changes as characters develop emotionally throughout the story.

## 5. Use Dialogue for Multiple Purposes

Great dialogue accomplishes several things simultaneously.

**Multi-purpose dialogue:**
- **Reveals character** while advancing plot
- **Provides information** without exposition
- **Creates tension** through conflict
- **Establishes mood** and atmosphere
- **Foreshadows future events**

**Example: Casablanca**
"Here's looking at you, kid" simultaneously reveals Rick's affection, establishes the film's romantic mood, and foreshadows their separation.

**Purpose tracking in Aevon:**
Use **Chapter Content** to note what each dialogue scene accomplishes, ensuring every conversation serves multiple purposes.

## 6. Read Dialogue Aloud

Your ear will catch problems your eyes miss.

**What to listen for:**
- **Natural rhythm** and flow
- **Awkward phrasing** or unrealistic speech
- **Character voice consistency**
- **Pacing and tension**
- **Emotional authenticity**

**Reading technique:**
1. Read dialogue without attribution tags
2. Can you tell who's speaking?
3. Does it sound like real people talking?
4. Are there sections that feel forced or unnatural?

**Aevon workflow:**
Use the **Quick Scratchpad** for dialogue testing. Copy conversations there and read them aloud before moving to the main manuscript.

## 7. Master Attribution Tags

Dialogue tags should be invisible, not intrusive.

**Best practices:**
- **Use "said"** most of the time—it's invisible to readers
- **Avoid adverbs** in tags (show emotion in dialogue instead)
- **Use action beats** instead of tags when possible
- **Vary tag placement** (beginning, middle, end)
- **Keep tags simple** and consistent

**Example:**
Instead of: "I'm leaving," she said angrily.
Try: "I'm leaving." She slammed the door behind her.

**Tag management in Aevon:**
Use the **Rich Text Editor** to experiment with different attribution styles. Use **Find and Replace** to check for overused adverbs or awkward tags.

## 8. Create Conflict and Tension

Boring dialogue has no conflict. Every conversation should have some tension.

**Sources of dialogue tension:**
- **Opposing goals**: Characters want different things
- **Information imbalance**: One character knows something the other doesn't
- **Power dynamics**: Authority figures vs. subordinates
- **Emotional stakes**: Characters care deeply about the outcome
- **Time pressure**: Urgency creates natural tension

**Example: A Few Good Men**
"Did you order the code red?" creates tension through opposing goals, power dynamics, and life-or-death stakes.

**Tension building in Aevon:**
Use the **Conflict field** in Character Attributes to track what creates tension between specific characters and how that affects their dialogue.

## 9. Use Dialect and Accents Sparingly

Dialect can make characters memorable, but overdone it becomes caricature.

**Dialect guidelines:**
- **Less is more**—suggest rather than imitate
- **Focus on rhythm** and word choice, not phonetic spelling
- **Be consistent** once established
- **Consider your audience** and potential offense
- **Use for characterization**, not decoration

**Example: Mark Twain**
Twain suggests dialect through word choice and rhythm rather than phonetic spelling: "I doan' take no stock in dat."

**Dialect tracking in Aevon:**
Use **Character Notes** to document dialect patterns and ensure consistency throughout the manuscript.

## 10. Include Silence and Pauses

What characters DON'T say matters as much as what they do.

**Using silence effectively:**
- **Ellipses** (...) for trailing thoughts
- **Em dashes** (—) for interruptions or sudden stops
- **Paragraph breaks** for significant pauses
- **Action beats** instead of dialogue
- **Internal thoughts** during silence

**Example: Hemingway**
Hemingway's "iceberg theory" uses subtext and silence extensively. What's unsaid creates more tension than what's spoken.

**Silence techniques in Aevon:**
Use the **Rich Text Editor** to experiment with different types of pauses and their effects on dialogue pacing.

## 11. Vary Sentence Length and Structure

Monotonous dialogue makes readers tune out.

**Varying dialogue patterns:**
- **Short, punchy lines** for action or emotion
- **Long, complex sentences** for explanation or thought
- **Questions and answers** for information exchange
- **Fragments and run-ons** for character voice
- **Mixed lengths** for natural rhythm

**Example: The Catcher in the Rye**
Holden Caulfield's dialogue mixes short, direct questions with longer, rambling thoughts, creating authentic teenage voice.

**Structure variation in Aevon:**
Use the **Ideas Workspace** to brainstorm different dialogue patterns for each character based on their personality and situation.

## 12. Edit Ruthlessly

First draft dialogue is rarely final dialogue.

**Editing checklist:**
- **Cut unnecessary words** and filler
- **Strengthen weak verbs** and vague descriptions
- **Ensure each line** serves a purpose
- **Check voice consistency** throughout
- **Read aloud one more time** for flow

**Editing process in Aevon:**
1. **Draft** dialogue in the Manuscript Editor
2. **Copy** to Quick Scratchpad for testing
3. **Edit** based on checklist
4. **Move** polished dialogue back to manuscript
5. **Track** changes using version history

## Common Dialogue Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. As-you-know-Bob Syndrome
Avoid having characters tell each other things they already know for the reader's benefit.

### 2. All Characters Sound the Same
If you remove dialogue tags, can you still tell who's speaking?

### 3. Too Much Exposition
Dialogue should reveal character, not dump information.

### 4. Unrealistic Formality
Most people don't speak in perfectly grammatical sentences.

### 5. Over-attribution
Too many dialogue tags and adverbs distract readers.

2. **Document** speech patterns, vocabulary, and verbal tics
3. **Practice** writing character voices in Quick Scratchpad

### Phase 2: Scene Planning
1. **Use** Ideas Workspace to plan dialogue scenes

3. **Map** sources of tension and conflict

1. **Move** polished dialogue back to manuscript
2. **Check** consistency with character development
3. **Verify** subtext and emotional authenticity
4. **Final read-aloud** for flow and rhythm

## Advanced Dialogue Techniques

### The Unreliable Narrator
Characters who lie, mislead, or have biased perspectives create tension through unreliable dialogue.

**Aevon implementation:** Use Character Notes to track what characters believe vs. what's true.

### Dialogue as Action
Sometimes what characters do while talking is more important than what they say.

**Aevon workflow:** Use Rich Text Editor to integrate action beats seamlessly with dialogue.

### Cultural and Generational Differences
Different backgrounds create natural dialogue variation through vocabulary, references, and communication styles.

**Aevon tools:** Document cultural background in Character Attributes to ensure consistent dialogue patterns.

## Testing Your Dialogue

Before finalizing dialogue, ask:

### The Voice Test
Can readers tell who's speaking without attribution tags?

### The Natural Test
Does the dialogue sound like real people talking?

### The Purpose Test
Does every line serve the story and characters?

### The Tension Test
Is there appropriate conflict and emotional stakes?

## Conclusion

Great dialogue makes your fictional world feel real, your characters believable, and your story compelling. By listening to real conversations, giving each character a unique voice, using subtext effectively, showing emotions through words, mastering attribution tags, creating tension, and editing ruthlessly, you'll craft dialogue that readers will remember and quote.

The best dialogue reveals character while advancing plot, creates tension while providing information, and sounds natural while serving the story. It's a delicate balance, but mastering it will elevate your writing from good to great.

Remember: dialogue is not just what characters say—it's who they are when they speak.
